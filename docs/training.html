Architecture Decision

Why two training backends (three if you count Gazebo)

A single Isaac Sim environment takes minutes to spin up and requires a GPU. Iterating on reward shaping, action spaces, and staged task decomposition in that environment is slow. Instead we used separate backends to explore different levels of control and training throughput:

BackendSpeedPurposeOwner
MuJoCo + SB3~1500 steps/s (CPU)Fast reward shaping, staged policy design, Jacobian IKJohn Chen
Gazebo + SB3~hundreds steps/s (CPU)Phase A reach baseline, joint limit tuningGuy Smith
Isaac Lab RSL-RL50k+ steps/s (GPU)Final scale training, 4096 parallel envsSebastian Baudille

The original goal was a shared obs/action contract, but the final workflow was more practical: MuJoCo was used for fast staged-policy experiments, while Isaac Sim trained in parallel from a similar PPO design direction. The similar convergence was a design alignment result, not a direct MuJoCo-to-Isaac weight transfer.

MuJoCo Backend — John Chen

Staged sub-policies (MuJoCo + SB3)

Rather than training a single policy to learn the entire pick-and-place sequence, John designed a staged architecture with three specialised sub-policies. Each learns a narrow, well-defined task it can converge on, rather than a complex sequence it might never learn from scratch.

Why staged policies

A monolithic reach→grasp→transport policy has an enormous state space and very sparse rewards (only the final place is rewarded). Early experiments in Gazebo showed 0% success at 300k steps with a joint-state-blind observation. Staged policies solve this by giving each sub-policy a dense reward for its specific sub-task, letting each converge quickly before being chained.

Coordinator state machine

A MuJoCoCoordinator chains the stages: PAN → EXTEND → GRASP → TRANSPORT → RELEASE. Each stage runs until its done condition is met, then hands off to the next. This made the MuJoCo branch more hardcoded than Isaac Sim, but it reduced the learning load enough to tune each component independently.

Key design decisions

  • Observation reduction: the first Gazebo Classic PPO setup used a 29D observation vector; after feedback about redundancy, the Ignition Fortress port reduced this to roughly 15D
  • Network tuning: MuJoCo reach experiments moved from 64x64 to 128x128 and finally 256x256 for the final panlockedreach file; pan stayed 64x64 because it was effectively 1D
  • Home pose at all-zeros: TCP starts near cube height instead of 70 cm above table, giving the pan policy a meaningful starting configuration
  • Orientation constraint removed from IK: arm uses full joint range for reaching; constraint removed to allow more reachable configurations
  • ctrl set during reset: position actuators hold home pose during physics settle phase so the arm starts stable
  • Renderer reuse: single mujoco.Renderer instance (not reallocated per render() call) — avoids GPU memory leaks in long training runs
  • Multiprocessing via spawn: not fork, to avoid MuJoCo CUDA memory sharing issues across workers

MJCF scene design

  • Floor, table at (-0.031, 0, 0.29) — matches Gazebo launch parameters
  • 4 free-body cubes with realistic physics (mass, friction)
  • 2 bin sites for pick-and-place target
  • 6 position actuators (shoulder_pan, shoulder_lift, elbow, wrist_1, wrist_2, wrist_3)
  • UR3e URDF expanded with base_z=1.10, base_yaw=π (matches Gazebo launch)
  • RG2 gripper: all fixed joints, virtual gripper_closed boolean — no physics actuation needed for staged RL
  • Table top at z=1.07 m = TABLE_TOP_Z constant shared with Gazebo env
Interface lesson: Keeping obs/action spaces compatible is still valuable, but we did not complete a direct MuJoCo-to-Isaac deployment path. Isaac Sim is increasingly the better long-term backend because it trains far more efficiently while keeping more robot DOF.

Jacobian IK — John Chen

Damped-least-squares Jacobian IK

A damped-least-squares Jacobian IK module converts 3D Cartesian target positions (dx, dy, dz) to absolute joint angle targets. This is used to seed the policy with valid configurations near the goal cube, so the agent begins training with the arm in a reachable pose rather than a random one.

Why IK seeding matters

Without IK seeding, early training episodes spent most of their time thrashing the arm in configurations that could never reach the cube. The agent saw zero reward for hundreds of thousands of steps. With seeding, the policy sees rewarding configurations from episode 1 and converges significantly faster.

IK details

  • Damping term prevents singularity blow-up near degenerate configurations
  • Orientation correction term keeps the gripper pointing straight down for top-down grasps
  • Joint limits clamped after each IK step to prevent out-of-range solutions
  • 5 unit tests: cache building, output shape/dtype, joint limit clamping, directional movement, zero-action stability

ONNX export prototype

The MuJoCo sub-policies can be exported to ONNX (opset 17, float32, deterministic inference), but this was a prototype compatibility path rather than the final Isaac Sim training route. Sebastian's Isaac Sim work trained in parallel from similar PPO weighting and parameters.

# Export all sub-policies to ONNX python3 export_onnx.py \ --pan rl_models/pan/best/best_model.zip \ --extend rl_models/extend/best/best_model.zip \ --grasp rl_models/grasp/best/best_model.zip \ --out /tmp/ur3e_policies # Output: pan.onnx [1,2]->[1,1] extend.onnx [1,5]->[1,3] grasp.onnx [1,5]->[1,3]

Gazebo Phase A — Gazebo + SB3

Gazebo PPO iteration path

Before the MuJoCo and Isaac Sim backends were established, the PPO work ran through Gazebo. The first version used Gazebo Classic with a large 29D observation vector. After feedback that the observation space was probably too large and redundant, John ported the setup to Ignition Fortress and reduced the state to roughly 15D. Ollie continued iterating on that reduced Gazebo setup, but the resulting motion quality still was not good enough to treat it as the final approach.

What changed from the initial attempt

The initial policy failed at 0% success after 300k steps. One root cause was that the observation did not provide enough useful robot state. Adding joint positions and reducing redundant variables helped the reach metric, but did not fully fix the control-quality problem.

Joint limit tightening (self-collision mitigation)

Some runs produced high reach metrics, but the arm still exhibited bad movement: upper arm sweeping through workspace, arm folding into itself, and self-collision-like behaviour. Tightening joint limits and adding a soft config penalty helped, but this was part of why the team moved toward MuJoCo sub-policies and Isaac Sim parallel training.

  • shoulder_lift: upper bound → −0.2 rad (keeps upper arm elevated, prevents horizontal sweep)
  • elbow: lower bound → −2.5 rad (prevents extreme reverse fold)
  • wrist_1: upper bound → 0 rad (prevents flip past neutral)
  • Config penalty: soft ramp 0 → −2.0 as shoulder_lift approaches upper limit — gives gradient signal away from the hard limit
Note: Changing joint limits invalidates the observation normalisation from Phase A. Any checkpoint trained with old limits is incompatible and must be retrained.

Isaac Lab — Sebastian Baudille

Evaluation

Results, successes & challenges

Successes

  • Observation reduction: feedback on the 29D Gazebo Classic observation vector led to an Ignition Fortress port with roughly 15D observations
  • Staged MuJoCo policies: breaking the task into pan and reach-style sub-policies made the training load manageable after the reduced Gazebo setup still produced poor movement quality
  • Network tuning: MuJoCo experiments scaled from 64x64 to 128x128 and then 256x256 for panlockedreach, while the 1D pan policy stayed at 64x64
  • Isaac Sim parallel training: Sebastian's Isaac Sim runs did not load MuJoCo weights, but converged toward similar behaviour from similar PPO weighting and parameter intent
  • Training throughput: Isaac Lab is becoming the stronger backend because thousands of parallel environments train much more efficiently than CPU-only Gazebo or MuJoCo loops

Challenges

  • Large observation vector
    Pengshuo and Louis both flagged that the 29D observation space was probably too large and redundant. Reducing it helped, but did not fully solve the motion-quality problem in Gazebo.
  • Robot movement quality
    Augustine's feedback matched what we saw in training: the robot movement was weird. Raw joint-delta PPO made smooth control difficult, and trajectory-level actions or MoveIt-style planning may be better for parts of the task.
  • Teleoperation data scale
    Raphael noted that training from teleoperation is hard because the amount of demonstration data is limited. Teleop data is useful, but it was not enough by itself to solve policy learning.
  • Hardcoding vs DOF
    MuJoCo sub-policies reduced complexity, but they also encoded more task structure than Isaac Sim. Isaac Sim is less scripted and keeps more robot DOF, which makes it more promising as training throughput improves.

Limitations & future work

  • Full pick-and-place (reach → grasp → transport → place) was not demonstrated as one deployed end-to-end policy
  • The MuJoCo approach may be less useful than MoveIt-style planning for deterministic motion segments, even if it is useful for fast PPO experiments
  • Learned pose models or learned features could replace PCA/SVM/classical extraction, but only if the hardware budget can support them
  • Domain randomisation available in Isaac Lab is promising but not yet tuned for sim-to-real transfer
