Architecture Decision

Why two training backends (three if you count Gazebo)

A single Isaac Sim environment takes minutes to spin up and requires a GPU. Iterating on reward shaping, action spaces, and staged task decomposition in that environment is slow. Instead we used backends with a shared obs/action contract:

BackendSpeedPurposeOwner
MuJoCo + SB3~1500 steps/s (CPU)Fast reward shaping, staged policy design, Jacobian IKJohn Chen
Gazebo + SB3~hundreds steps/s (CPU)Phase A reach baseline, joint limit tuningGuy Smith
Isaac Lab RSL-RL50k+ steps/s (GPU)Final scale training, 4096 parallel envsSebastian Baudille

The key constraint: obs/action spaces must be identical across backends so trained weights transfer. MuJoCo sub-policies are exported to ONNX and loaded into Isaac Sim for deployment.

MuJoCo Backend — John Chen

Staged sub-policies (MuJoCo + SB3)

Rather than training a single policy to learn the entire pick-and-place sequence, John designed a staged architecture with three specialised sub-policies. Each learns a narrow, well-defined task it can converge on, rather than a complex sequence it might never learn from scratch.

Why staged policies

A monolithic reach→grasp→transport policy has an enormous state space and very sparse rewards (only the final place is rewarded). Early experiments in Gazebo showed 0% success at 300k steps with a joint-state-blind observation. Staged policies solve this by giving each sub-policy a dense reward for its specific sub-task, letting each converge quickly before being chained.

Coordinator state machine

A MuJoCoCoordinator chains the stages: PAN → EXTEND → GRASP → TRANSPORT → RELEASE. Each stage runs until its done condition is met, then hands off to the next. The coordinator is responsible for loading each sub-policy’s ONNX weights and managing the joint action/obs routing for whichever stage is active.

Key design decisions

  • Home pose at all-zeros: TCP starts near cube height instead of 70 cm above table, giving the pan policy a meaningful starting configuration
  • Orientation constraint removed from IK: arm uses full joint range for reaching; constraint removed to allow more reachable configurations
  • ctrl set during reset: position actuators hold home pose during physics settle phase so the arm starts stable
  • Renderer reuse: single mujoco.Renderer instance (not reallocated per render() call) — avoids GPU memory leaks in long training runs
  • Multiprocessing via spawn: not fork, to avoid MuJoCo CUDA memory sharing issues across workers

MJCF scene design

  • Floor, table at (-0.031, 0, 0.29) — matches Gazebo launch parameters
  • 4 free-body cubes with realistic physics (mass, friction)
  • 2 bin sites for pick-and-place target
  • 6 position actuators (shoulder_pan, shoulder_lift, elbow, wrist_1, wrist_2, wrist_3)
  • UR3e URDF expanded with base_z=1.10, base_yaw=π (matches Gazebo launch)
  • RG2 gripper: all fixed joints, virtual gripper_closed boolean — no physics actuation needed for staged RL
  • Table top at z=1.07 m = TABLE_TOP_Z constant shared with Gazebo env
Weight transfer guarantee: The MuJoCo env observation and action spaces are defined identically to the Gazebo env. A policy trained in MuJoCo can be loaded directly into Gazebo or exported via ONNX to Isaac Sim with no architecture changes.

Jacobian IK — John Chen

Damped-least-squares Jacobian IK

A damped-least-squares Jacobian IK module converts 3D Cartesian target positions (dx, dy, dz) to absolute joint angle targets. This is used to seed the policy with valid configurations near the goal cube, so the agent begins training with the arm in a reachable pose rather than a random one.

Why IK seeding matters

Without IK seeding, early training episodes spent most of their time thrashing the arm in configurations that could never reach the cube. The agent saw zero reward for hundreds of thousands of steps. With seeding, the policy sees rewarding configurations from episode 1 and converges significantly faster.

IK details

  • Damping term prevents singularity blow-up near degenerate configurations
  • Orientation correction term keeps the gripper pointing straight down for top-down grasps
  • Joint limits clamped after each IK step to prevent out-of-range solutions
  • 5 unit tests: cache building, output shape/dtype, joint limit clamping, directional movement, zero-action stability

ONNX export for Isaac Sim

All three sub-policies are exported to ONNX (opset 17, float32, deterministic inference) so Sebastian can load them into Isaac Sim without needing a Python SB3 environment. Inputs/outputs all in [−1, 1]. Actions are delta joint angles, not absolute positions.

# Export all sub-policies to ONNX python3 export_onnx.py \ --pan rl_models/pan/best/best_model.zip \ --extend rl_models/extend/best/best_model.zip \ --grasp rl_models/grasp/best/best_model.zip \ --out /tmp/ur3e_policies # Output: pan.onnx [1,2]->[1,1] extend.onnx [1,5]->[1,3] grasp.onnx [1,5]->[1,3]

Gazebo Phase A — Guy Smith

Phase A reach training (99% success)

Before the MuJoCo and Isaac Sim backends were established, Guy ran Phase A training directly in Gazebo to prove the reach task was solvable and identify what observation/reward design worked.

What changed from the initial attempt

The initial policy failed at 0% success after 300k steps. The root cause: the observation didn’t include joint positions. The agent could see where the cube was but had no idea where its own arm was, making it impossible to plan a trajectory.

Phase A design (converged at 696k steps, 99% success)

ComponentDesignWhy
Observation (14D)EE XYZ + cube XYZ + 6 joints + height + timestepAdding joint positions was the fix that unlocked learning
Action (6D)Joint deltas, no gripperGripper not needed for reach; removing it reduces policy noise
Reward−0.3×dist + time penalty + smoothness + +5.0 terminal reach bonusDense guidance + terminal sparse bonus

Joint limit tightening (self-collision fix)

Phase A hit 99% success but the arm exhibited self-collision: upper arm sweeping through workspace, arm folding into itself. Guy tightened joint limits and added a soft config penalty:

  • shoulder_lift: upper bound → −0.2 rad (keeps upper arm elevated, prevents horizontal sweep)
  • elbow: lower bound → −2.5 rad (prevents extreme reverse fold)
  • wrist_1: upper bound → 0 rad (prevents flip past neutral)
  • Config penalty: soft ramp 0 → −2.0 as shoulder_lift approaches upper limit — gives gradient signal away from the hard limit
Note: Changing joint limits invalidates the observation normalisation from Phase A. Any checkpoint trained with old limits is incompatible and must be retrained.

Isaac Lab — Sebastian Baudille

Sebastian built the Isaac Lab training environment using the DirectRLEnv API with modular strategy pattern. 4096 environments run in parallel on a single RTX 4070 Ti SUPER. See the Isaac Sim docs page for full architecture detail — this section covers training outcomes only.

Grab task results

Grab v0 achieved 97% success rate (196/200 mean reward) on a 5 cm lift threshold, with self-collision physics disabled. Reward versions v1–v4 each uncovered a new exploitation pattern. v5 (conservative return to v0’s scales + self-collision) is ready to train.

Evaluation

Results, successes & challenges

Successes

  • Phase A (Gazebo): 99% reach success at 696k steps — proves the observation/reward design works
  • Staged architecture: each sub-policy converges independently; the coordinator chains them without additional training
  • IK seeding: meaningfully accelerated convergence by eliminating degenerate early episodes
  • ONNX pipeline: clean handoff from MuJoCo (CPU training) to Isaac Sim (GPU deployment) with no Python environment dependency
  • Grab v0: 97% success rate — proves the grab task is solvable with the Isaac Lab architecture

Challenges

  • Self-collision in Gazebo
    The collision checker never triggered reliably during training. Fixed by tightening joint limits and adding gradient-signal config penalty instead of relying on binary collision flags.
  • Isaac Lab partially converged on reach
    60–70% reach success after 500 iterations. Needs ~1500 total iterations and entropy coefficient tuning. The reward curve is improving monotonically, so this is a compute/time issue, not a design problem.
  • Grab reward exploitation (5 iterations)
    Each of v1–v4 uncovered a new policy exploit: hover trap, hold-below-threshold, finger drag, and safe hover. v5 returns to the only proven configuration (v0) and changes exactly one thing at a time.
  • Obs/action space compatibility
    MuJoCo and Isaac Lab envs must have identical spaces for weight transfer. Maintaining this contract across three independently developed backends required careful coordination.

Limitations & future work

  • Full pick-and-place (reach → grasp → transport → place) not yet demonstrated end-to-end — reach phase converged; grasp and transport in progress
  • Domain randomisation available in Isaac Lab (built-in) but not yet tuned for sim-to-real transfer
  • ONNX sub-policies need integration into a live Isaac Sim deployment node that feeds real camera centroids from the DBSCAN pipeline
